Part 1: Masses and Springs

In this part, we implement a cloth using the model of a system of point masses and springs connecting them. We first evenly divide the cloth, whose size is defined by the parameters width and height, into a grid of point masses, which we store in a 1D array. Then we make sure to pin the correct point masses given by the cloth’s parameters. Then we create the springs that connect the point masses and create the tension that holds the cloth together. We apply the structural, shearing, and bending constraints to the point masses as appropriate, while being sure to handle the edge cases for point masses on the boundary of the grid.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, we calculate the forces acting on the cloth’s point masses to simulate how they move over time. We need to account for both external forces (gravity) and spring correction forces, which are the forces resulting from the stretching/compression of the springs connecting the point masses of the cloth.

First we compute the total force acting on each point mass. The external forces apply uniformly to the cloth as a whole, so for each point mass we can just apply the external force from the external accelerations using F = ma. Then, we add the spring correction forces calculated using Hooke’s law. We iterate through the list of springs, and if that spring’s constraint type is enabled, we calculate and add the appropriate forces to the two point masses that the spring is connecting.

Once we have computed the force acting on each point mass at this time step, we need to use numerical integration to compute where the point mass will be at the next time step. We use Verlet integration to compute these new positions. We also include a damping term to simulate loss of energy due to friction. Finally, we ensure that we do not update the position of pinned point masses.

Finally, we add a deformation constraint to stop springs from being unreasonably deformed in each time step. We cap the max distance between the two point masses (length of the spring) at 1.1x the spring’s rest length, modifying the point masses’ positions if necessary.

Cloth’s resting state with default parameters

The spring constant ks controls how stiff the springs that connect the point masses in the cloth are. A small value of ks means that the springs are very springy. In this case, the cloth is very elastic and flexible and has many small wrinkles as it falls. With a high value of ks, the springs are very stiff and thus the cloth becomes much less elastic and more rigid. It is as if the cloth is made of a thicker, stiffer material.

The density controls the weight of the cloth. With a low density, the cloth is lighter and falls more freely. In its resting position, there is only a single shallow fold at the center of the cloth. With a high density, the cloth is being pulled down by its weight, which results in many more deep folds as the cloth bows down under its weight as it is falling. At the resting position, the fold at the middle of the cloth is much deeper than in the low density case.

The damping constant controls how much of the cloth’s energy in each time step is dissipated due to friction, heat loss, etc. With a very low damping value, the cloth loses very little energy and thus continues oscillating back and forth like a pendulum and does not come to a resting position. With a very high damping value, the cloth loses much of its energy on its initial fall and thus does not oscillate past the neutral position at all.

With no damping, the cloth loses no energy and swings all the way to a horizontal position on the other side

Part 3: Collisions with other objects

In this part, we implement the ability for the cloth to collide with other objects in the scene (spheres and planes). At each time step, for each point mass, we have to check for collisions with all the other objects in the scene.

For spheres, if a point mass is now intersecting with or inside the sphere (distance from point mass to center of sphere is less than or equal to radius), we bump it back up to the surface of the sphere. We find what would have been the intersection point and move the point mass towards that tangent point, scaled down by friction.

For planes, if a point mass is on the opposite side of the plane as it was on the previous time step, then we know there was a collision. Similarly, we find the intersection point (plus a small SURFACE_OFFSET) and move the point mass towards this point, with the correction scaled down by friction.

Part 4: Self-collisions

In this part, we implement self-collisions so that the cloth does not just clip through itself. Rather than naively checking all pairs of point masses for collisions, we use spatial hashing to improve performance. We build a hash table of the point masses based on their 3D position so that we only have to check point masses within the same 3D box. We implemented a hash function that maps each 3D box to a unique float, which we use as the key for the map of point masses. Thus, at each time step, for each point mass we can efficiently find all the other point masses that share its 3D box. Then, for each point mass, if any of the other point masses in its box are colliding with it (distance less than 2*thickness), we add a correction vector to push them far enough apart. Then, the final correction vector is just the average of all the pairwise correction vectors, scaled down by simulation_steps for accuracy.

When we change ks to 500 (make the cloth very elastic), the cloth has many small folds as it falls. When we change ks to 50000 (make the cloth very stiff), the cloth has very few but large folds as it falls.

Part 5: Shaders

In this part we implemented shaders. Shaders are programs that run in parallel on GPUs and execute specific parts of the graphics pipeline. Vertex shaders are applied to vertices and modify their geometric properties like position and normal vectors. Fragment shaders process the fragments that we get after rasterization, and take in the geometric attributes from the vertex shader to compute and output the colors that are displayed on screen.

In the Blinn-Phong shading model we account for ambient lighting, diffuse lighting, and specular highlights, and sum these terms to compute the total lighting.

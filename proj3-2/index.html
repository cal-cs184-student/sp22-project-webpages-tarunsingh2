Part 1

        The implementation for mirror materials is straightforward. We simply reflect the incoming ray about the surface normal (always <0, 0, 1> in object space). To be able to render glass materials, we also need to account for rays that are refracted through the glass. We implement the refraction function using Snell’s law. We need to determine whether the ray is entering the glass from air or exiting the glass out into air, as this changes the value of eta used in Snell’s law. We also need to check for total internal reflection, in which case the ray is not actually refracted into the second medium, but completely reflected back into the first medium. Since glass has both reflection and refraction, we need to know how much of each occurs. We use Schlick’s approximation to calculate this ratio and choose between reflection and refraction with this probability.

 

With max_ray_depth = 0, we only see rays emanating directly from the light source.

With max_ray_depth = 1, we see objects that are directly illuminated by the light source. Rays are emanating from the light and bouncing off the walls and floor toward the camera, so we see these areas illuminated. We also see shadows on the floor in the areas that are occluded from the light source by the spheres. Finally, because the spheres are glossy, there are rays from the light source bouncing off the spheres toward the camera, so we see the glare on the spheres.

With max_ray_depth = 2, we are now also rendering indirect illumination. Light is bouncing off objects back towards the ceiling and then towards the camera, so the ceiling is now illuminated. We can now see the reflection of the room in the mirror sphere. However, because this takes an extra bounce, we are seeing the room as it was “one bounce ago”. For example, in the mirror sphere the ceiling appears dark. In the glass sphere, we can also now see a reflection of the floor.

With max_ray_depth = 3, we can now see the glass ball refracting light. For instance, light is emanating from the light source, bouncing off the blue wall (1), entering the glass ball (2), and then exiting the glass ball towards the camera (3). We can now also see the light refracted by the glass ball onto the floor, which emanates from the light source, enters the glass ball (1), exits the glass ball (2), then bounces off the floor towards the camera. Again, the mirror ball is “one bounce behind” so the glass ball still appears dark inside the mirror ball.

With max_ray_depth = 4, the light that was refracted onto the floor by the glass ball is reflecting back off the glass ball towards the camera, which creates a highlight on the bottom of the glass ball. The glass ball is also now refracting light onto the blue wall.

With max_ray_depth = 5, the image looks very similar to the previous one, since most of the multibounce effects occur within the first few bounces.

Again, with max_ray_depth = 100 the image looks very similar. The prominent multibounce effects have already occurred, plus with Russian Roulette termination most rays are not allowed to bounce for anywhere near 100 times anyway.
